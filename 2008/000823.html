The ancient ELF logs are one column.
The old ELF logs are 2 columns.
And the new ELF logs are 3 columns.

What are the columns?

# cat /etc/sorcery/log/elf/zlib
/lib/libz.so    /lib/libc.so.6  glibc
/lib/libz.so.1  /lib/libc.so.6  glibc
/lib/libz.so.1.2.3      /lib/libc.so.6  glibc
/usr/lib/libz.so        /lib/libc.so.6  glibc
/usr/lib/libz.so.1      /lib/libc.so.6  glibc
/usr/lib/libz.so.1.2.3  /lib/libc.so.6  glibc


The first column is the ELF installed by the spell.
The second column is an ELF required by the ELF installed by the spell.
The third column is the spell that owns the ELF in the 2nd column.

Confused yet?
No problem.
Understanding ELF logs is not necessary nor required.

The 3 column format grants much faster responses to:

augur arof
augur aron
augur check

Spell authors use those functions.
I use augur check for each new version of a spell I check.

The boons are the sweet speed we crave and enjoy.
And the cost?

/etc/sorcery/log/elf/ grows between about 10% to 15%

Typical workstation:

# du -sm /etc/sorcery/log/* | sort -g | tail -n 4
12      /etc/sorcery/log/install
14      /etc/sorcery/log/elf
14      /etc/sorcery/log/md5sum
15      /etc/sorcery/log/compile

# du -sm /etc/sorcery/log
64      /etc/sorcery/log

64M is a tiny drop in the bucket compared to 30G and larger fixed disks.
64M is still very reasonable size for the unmitigated speed
from accessing most of that data without compression.
If all the log files were individually compressed
they would probably occupy 10M or less.
But I want speed!
How about you?
