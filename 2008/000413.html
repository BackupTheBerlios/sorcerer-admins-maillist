The initramfs spell which is installed on all new deployments of Sorcerer
now prompts for whether the /boot/*/initrf should be gzip compressed or not.
gzip compression requires a bit longer to create and saves about 20M
The gzip compressed initrf requires about 30M and without it 50M
However, the 20M savings is not significant on boxes with a large enough /boot
or on boxes without a dedicated /boot
That is why compressing the initrf is optional.
If /boot is less than 256M compressing may be required in order to store
4 kernels 4 maps and 4 initrf on /boot

All modules linux kernels modules are now gzip compressed.
modprobe and udev automatically gunzip compress modules while loading
those modules.
The reason for this is that modules require a large amount of space.
But few are actually loaded.
While remaining gzip compressed the modules take up less space on the initramfs
and less RAM while a box is booting.
And the time to gunzip compressed modules is insignificant.
Therefore, gzip compressed modules are a benefit worth keeping.

gzip compressed modules has been the norm for a few months already.
There is nothing to be done about it.
However, remember to answer yes or no to the query to compress initrf images
the next time initramfs casts.
Otherwise, by default, it will not gzip compress /boot/*/initrf


Again I remind everyone that Install/Rescue disks that are
over 1 year old are useless for rescue.
Yes, they boot.
However, after mounting the fixed disk partition it will not be
possible to chroot into it.
The files on an updated box will require a newer linux kernel to be
running than the kernel
that loaded from the I/R.
Therefore, even if you are not installing Sorcerer today
be sure to download the new 20080501 I/Rs ASAP
and burn them to disk so that you will not be without an
Rescue disk if and when a box requires a rescue.
