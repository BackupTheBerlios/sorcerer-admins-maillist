It already works in this way internally. It also does dependency resolution
and optimization for your machine. There is even a menu for Sorcerer. Just
type sorcery at the command line, and press enter.

However, this is not all that is required for popularity, and being popular
has never been a goal for Sorcerer.
In fact, there are only very few people that run Sorcerer on a daily basis.

There are many reasons for this, but I will touch on the first few that pop
into my mind.

1. Nothing is configured for you in Sorcerer. You are expected to read the
manual of the software you are installing, and configure it yourself.

2. The license. Sorcerer is free to use, and copy, but you cannot fork it
like you can with GPL code. It belongs to Kyle Sallee, and due to reasons
that will take too long in this mail to explain, that is not about to
change. I highly suggest reading the license if you ever install Sorcerer.

3. It's unstable. Sorcerer is permanently in a state of flux. Some days are
better than others as far as software compiling, installing and running
correctly goes. We are permanently messing with the way Sorcerer works, and
that may stop it working correctly. However, you have our solemn promise
that this is not intentional, and that we will try our best to squash any
bugs that are brought to our attention.

So, why run Sorcerer at all?
1. Freedom. You can configure your software the way you like it. You learn
how it works, and use the software to it's full effect.

2. It's by far the most elegant distrobution as far as software handling is
concerned. Every year or so I try Gentoo, Funtoo or some Debian derivative.
They are bandwidth hogs, insult my intelligence and don't work the way I
do.

3. You learn how your computer work, and how to fix it. Also, there are
people running Sorcerer in production environments, but they don't just
blindly update, they have a machine that they test all updates on first
before throwing it on the production machine. This, and backups are just
good practice, no matter who wrote the software you are running.
