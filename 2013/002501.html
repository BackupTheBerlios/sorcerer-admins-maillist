>
> Of course I concur.
> Every aspect of the sorcery tools
> can be modified by editing /etc/sorcery/config.
> However, because it can be modified
> is not necessarily sufficient reason to modify it.
>
> About a decade or so we also were
> somewhat excited about compiler optimization.
> And then more recently it was about link time optimization.
> However, testing revealed that there was as much to gain
> as to lose from enabling it.
> Such things are not clear benefits.
> I can give detailed examples.
> However, in this rare situation I will omit doing so unless requested.
>
> "There comes a time, thief, when the jewels cease to sparkle,
> when the gold loses its luster, when the throne room becomes a prison,
> and all that is left is a father's love for his child."
> Ha, wait, no that's from the 1982 movie "Conan the Barbarian."
>
> There comes a time when the micromanagement loses its luster.
> And the POSIX becomes the like a subordinate SA.
> And all that is left is for the SA to do the tasks
> that require the skills of a SA such as
> editing configuration files for Apache httpd or
> editing configuration files for Berkeley Internet Name Domain.
> *chuckles*  :)
>
> With that said SAs are not encouraged to micromanage.
> Doing so merely invites in possibilities for bugs
> that would not be surmised when a bug report is submitted.
> Therefore, all extraordinary modifications should be reported
> along with bug reports when seeking help.
> Typically, the less extraordinary the modifications
> then the more extraordinary the performance.
>
> There is one exemption.
> If desired the selected processor governor can be performance.
> I use it from time to time.
> However, many of us tend to use the ondemand governor.
> That allows frequency of processors to be scaled up when demand exists.
> However, the adjustment is not instant.
> Performance is lost while using ondemand.
> However, power is conserved and less heat is produced.
>
> Not many of us dare to allow our computers
> to hibernate to RAM or hibernate to disk.
> Typically, hibernate to disk requires special provision
> for a sufficiently large swap partition during deployment.
> So the next best thing to do before logging out of a workstation
> or locking the screen is to switch to the powersave governor.
> At a command line type
> # cpufreq
> then press tab twice for a list of governors.
>
> If the frequency for processors is desired
> then at any time run:
> $ grep MHz /proc/cpuinfo
>
> # grep MHz /proc/cpuinfo
> cpu MHz         : 3000.000
> cpu MHz         : 3000.000
> cpu MHz         : 800.000
> cpu MHz         : 800.000
>
> Something like that might not be uncommon
> for the ondemand governor.
> And it could change to something else
> in less than a second.
>
> If we want to adjust performance
> then utilization of CPU frequency and governors
> tends to be how we do it these days.
>
> BTW, do not feel obliged to use my program
> to adjust the governors and frequency.
> The files can be accessed directly
> within the /sys/ hierarchy.
> They are somewhere within there.
> Might require using find and grep to locate.
> However, the greatest amount of flexibility
> is provided by adjusting them directly.
> The cpufreq program is merely a convenience tool.
